Jain Nav Tattva Principle: A Detailed Understanding

Introduction

In Jain philosophy, the knowledge of the nine Tattvas (principles) is considered extremely essential for the welfare of the soul and the attainment of liberation (Moksha). These nine Tattvas explain the nature of all substances in the universe and guide the soul to understand its true self, showing the path to freedom from the sufferings of the world. In this report, we will understand the principle of the Jain Nav Tattva in a beautiful and simple language, with its definitions, important points, and practical examples.

In the world, there are mainly two types of substances: Jiva (soul) and Ajiva (non-soul). To understand the interrelationship of these two fundamental Tattvas and the resulting consequences, the Omniscient Lords (Jinvar Parmatma) expounded the nine Tattvas. The accurate knowledge of these nine Tattvas and faith in them is indispensable for the attainment of Samyag Darshan (right faith), which is the first step on the path to Moksha.

The nine Tattvas are as follows:

  1. Jiva (Soul)
  2. Ajiva (Non-soul)
  3. Punya (Merit or good karma)
  4. Papa (Demerit or bad karma)
  5. Ashrava (Influx of karma)
  6. Samvara (Stoppage of karmic influx)
  7. Nirjara (Dissipation of karma)
  8. Bandha (Bondage of karma)
  9. Moksha (Complete liberation from karma)

These nine Tattvas are primarily viewed from three perspectives:

  • **Gneya (Worth knowing):** Jiva and Ajiva Tattvas.
  • **Upadeya (Worth accepting or adopting):** Punya, Samvara, Nirjara, and Moksha Tattvas.
  • **Heya (Worth abandoning):** Papa, Ashrava, and Bandha Tattvas.

However, from the perspective of Nishchaya Naya (real or transcendental viewpoint), Punya is also a type of Ashrava and thus worth abandoning. But from the perspective of Vyavahara Naya (practical or conventional viewpoint), Punya, in the form of Shubha Pravritti (auspicious activities), is considered Upadeya in the initial stages for progressing on the path of Dharma and avoiding inauspicious activities.

1. Jiva Tattva

Definition:

That which is characterized by consciousness, which lives, which possesses life forces (pranas), and is endowed with the utilization of knowledge and perception, is Jiva. Jiva is an eternal, beginningless, endless, and formless substance. Due to the conjunction with karmas, it becomes embodied and transmigrates through the four states of existence (Deva, Manushya, Tiryanch, Naraka) in the world.

Important Points:

  • The main characteristic of Jiva is **Upayoga (utilization of consciousness)**, which manifests as knowledge and perception. No Jiva is ever without Upayoga.
  • There are mainly 14 types of worldly Jivas, based on senses (indriya), bodies (kaya), complete development (paryapti), and mental faculty (sangya):
    • One-sensed (subtle and gross - complete/incomplete) - 4 types
    • Two-sensed (gross - complete/incomplete) - 2 types
    • Three-sensed (gross - complete/incomplete) - 2 types
    • Four-sensed (gross - complete/incomplete) - 2 types
    • Five-sensed (with mind and without mind - complete/incomplete) - 4 types
  • Jiva possesses 10 life forces (pranas): 5 senses (touch, taste, smell, sight, hearing), 3 powers (mind, speech, body), respiration, and lifespan.
  • Jiva has the potential to complete 6 development potentials (paryaptis): food, body, senses, respiration, speech, and mind. A Jiva that completes its potential developments is called Paryapta (complete), and one that dies before completing them is called Aparyapta (incomplete).
  • **Sangyi Jivas** are those with a mind and can think about what is beneficial and harmful, while **Asangyi Jivas** are without a mind.
  • **Sukshma Jivas (Subtle)** are not visible to the senses and are pervasive throughout the entire universe, while **Badar Jivas (Gross)** are perceptible to the senses.

Practical Examples:

  • The soul residing in our body is Jiva.
  • Humans, animals, birds, celestial beings (Devas), and infernal beings (Narakis) are different types of Jivas.
  • Earth, water, fire, air, and plants also contain Jivas, which are called one-sensed Jivas.
  • Organisms like conch shells, oysters, and earthworms are two-sensed.
  • Ants, termites, and bedbugs are three-sensed.
  • Mosquitoes, flies, and butterflies are four-sensed.
  • Humans, cows, lions, fish, Devas, and Narakis are five-sensed. Sangyi five-sensed beings can think, like humans.

2. Ajiva Tattva

Definition:

That which has no consciousness, no soul, and no life forces, and which does not experience pleasure or pain, is Ajiva. Ajiva is inanimate substance.

Important Points:

  • There are mainly five types of Ajiva Tattva, also called Dravyas (substances):
    • **Pudgalaastikaya:** That which has color, smell, taste, and touch. It fills up and dissolves, hence it is perishable. (Form-possessing substance)
    • **Dharmaastikaya:** Helps Jiva and Pudgala in movement. (Formless substance)
    • **Adharmaastikaya:** Helps Jiva and Pudgala in resting or stability. (Formless substance)
    • **Akashaastikaya:** Provides space for all substances to exist. (Formless substance)
    • **Kala (Time):** Helps in the change or modification of substances. It is non-extensive (apradēśī), hence not an Astikaya. (Formless substance)
  • Main qualities of Pudgala:
    • **Color (5):** Black, blue, red, yellow, white.
    • **Smell (2):** Fragrant, malodorous.
    • **Taste (5):** Pungent, bitter, astringent, sour, sweet.
    • **Touch (8):** Cold, hot, unctuous, dry, light, heavy, soft, rough.

Practical Examples:

  • All physical objects visible around us, such as tables, chairs, clothes, vehicles, buildings, etc., are Pudgala.
  • The body is also made of Pudgala.
  • An example of Dharmaastikaya is how water helps a fish to move.
  • An example of Adharmaastikaya is how the shade of a tree helps someone to rest.
  • An example of Akashaastikaya is getting space in a room to keep things.
  • An example of Kala is how things become old or change over time.

3. Punya Tattva

Definition:

Good karma is Punya. Due to its उदय (rise), the Jiva experiences happiness, favorable circumstances, and attains Dharma in the world.

Important Points:

  • Punya is not the inherent nature of the soul, but a type of good karma bound due to auspicious thoughts and activities.
  • There are mainly 42 types of Punya, which are bound due to Shubha Ashrava (auspicious influx).
  • Main causes of Punya Bandha (bondage):
    • Giving donations to worthy recipients.
    • Devotion to the Jina.
    • Serving Gurus and elders.
    • Auspicious activities of mind, speech, and body.
    • Having compassion for suffering beings and helping them.
    • Practicing the Dharma of compassion (Daya).

Practical Examples:

  • Helping a poor or needy person is Punya.
  • Performing worship and devotion in a temple is Punya.
  • Serving the Guru Maharaj is Punya.
  • Having good thoughts that benefit others, speaking good words, and doing good deeds is Punya.
  • Showing compassion to living beings is Punya.

4. Papa Tattva

Definition:

Inauspicious karma is Papa. Due to its उदय (rise), the Jiva experiences sorrow, unfavorable circumstances, and attains Adharma in the world.

Important Points:

  • Papa is a type of inauspicious karma bound due to Ashubha Ashrava (inauspicious influx).
  • There are mainly 82 types of Papa, among which the 18 Papa Sthanakas (places of sin) are prominent.
  • 18 Papa Sthanakas (Places where Papa resides):
    1. Himsa (Violence): Causing pain to any living being or destroying its life force.
    2. Asatya (Falsehood): Speaking lies.
    3. Chori (Theft): Taking someone's property without their permission.
    4. Maithuna (Sexual activity): Indulging in sexual desires.
    5. Parigraha (Possessiveness): Accumulating more things than necessary.
    6. Krodha (Anger): Becoming angry.
    7. Mana (Pride): Being arrogant.
    8. Maya (Deceit): Practicing cunningness or cheating.
    9. Lobha (Greed): Having excessive desire to acquire things.
    10. Raga (Attachment): Having excessive fondness for a desired object.
    11. Dvesha (Aversion): Having jealousy or dislike towards someone.
    12. Kalaha (Quarrel): Engaging in disputes.
    13. Abhyakhyana (Calumny): Making false accusations against someone.
    14. Paishunya (Tale-bearing): Gossiping about someone.
    15. Rati-Arati (Liking-Disliking): Being pleased with what is liked and displeased with what is disliked.
    16. Para Parivada (Criticism of others): Speaking ill of others.
    17. Maya Mrishavada (Deceitful Falsehood): Speaking lies with deceit.
    18. Mithyatva Shalya (Thorn of Wrong Belief): Having contrary faith in the Tattvas.

Practical Examples:

  • Killing an insect is Papa of Himsa.
  • Cheating in an exam is Papa of Chori.
  • Lying to deceive someone is Papa of Asatya and Maya.
  • Getting angry over small matters is Papa of Krodha.
  • Not being content with what one has and desiring to acquire more is Papa of Lobha.

5. Ashrava Tattva

Definition:

That through which the influx of karmas enters the soul is Ashrava. It is the cause of karma bondage.

Important Points:

  • Ashrava is the result of the Jiva's states like Mithyatva, Avirati, Kashaya, Yoga, and Pramada.
  • There are mainly 42 types of Ashrava.
  • The five main types of Ashrava (which are causes of karma bondage):
    • **Mithyatva (Wrong Belief):** Lack of faith in the true Dev, Guru, and Dharma, and faith in false Dev, Guru, and Adharma. Contrary belief about the Tattvas.
    • **Avirati (Non-abstinence):** Lack of self-control without vows or renunciations. (Non-abstinence towards the 5 senses and 6 types of Jivas).
    • **Kashaya (Passions):** Krodha (anger), Mana (pride), Maya (deceit), and Lobha (greed).
    • **Yoga (Activity):** Auspicious or inauspicious activity of mind, speech, and body.
    • **Pramada (Negligence):** Indifference towards Dharma, attachment to sense objects and passions, engaging in improper talks (vikatha), and sleep.

Practical Examples:

  • Just as water enters a boat through holes, so does the influx of karmas (like water) enter the soul through the holes of states like Mithyatva and Avirati.
  • Thinking of harming a living being (activity of mind), speaking about it (activity of speech), and actually harming it (activity of body) is Ashrava.
  • Holding wrong beliefs is Mithyatva Ashrava.
  • Being tempted by the objects of the senses is Avirati Ashrava.
  • Having bad thoughts about someone is Ashubha Yoga of mind, which is Ashrava.
  • Speaking harsh words to someone is Ashubha Yoga of speech, which is Ashrava.
  • Causing physical harm to someone is Ashubha Yoga of body, which is Ashrava.
  • Being lazy or inattentive in religious activities is Pramada Ashrava.

6. Samvara Tattva

Definition:

The inherent power of the soul to stop the incoming flow of karmas is Samvara. The cessation of passions like Raga (attachment) and Dvesha (aversion) is also Samvara.

Important Points:

  • Samvara is the opposite of Ashrava; it is the stoppage of karmic influx.
  • There are mainly 57 types of Samvara.
  • The main means to stop the karmic flow:
    • **Five Samitis (Carefulness):** Carefulness in walking (Irya), speaking (Bhasha), taking food (Eshana), handling things (Adana Bhandamatta Nikshepana), and disposing of waste (Parishthapanika).
    • **Three Guptis (Control):** Controlling or restraining the activities of mind, speech, and body.
    • **Twenty-two Parishahas (Endurances):** Maintaining equanimity in pleasant and unpleasant circumstances.
    • **Ten Yati Dharmas (Duties of Ascetics):** Forgiveness, humility, straightforwardness, contentment, purity, self-control, truthfulness, renunciation, non-possession (Akincanya), and celibacy.
    • **Twelve Bhavanas (Reflections):** Impermanence, helplessness, world, separateness, oneness, impurity, Ashrava, Samvara, Nirjara, nature of the universe, Dharma, and right enlightenment.
    • **Five Charitras (Conducts):** Samayika (equanimity), Chedopasthapaniya (re-establishing in vows after transgression), Parihara Vishuddhi (pure conduct with special austerity), Sukshma Samparaya (conduct with subtle passion), and Yathakhyata (ideal conduct free from all passions).

Practical Examples:

  • Just as holes in a boat are sealed to stop water from entering, Samvara stops the influx of karmas into the soul.
  • Not getting angry is Samvara of Krodha Kashaya.
  • Not speaking lies is Samvara of Asatya Ashrava.
  • Meditating is Samvara of the activities of mind and body.
  • Being careful while walking to avoid harming small creatures is Irya Samiti Samvara.
  • Always speaking truthfully and limitedly is Bhasha Samiti Samvara.
  • Restraining the mind from bad thoughts is Manogupti Samvara.
  • Not being disturbed by hunger and thirst is Parishaha Samvara.

7. Nirjara Tattva

Definition:

The inherent power of the soul to destroy the karmas that are in existence and those that come into उदय (rise) is Nirjara. It is the shedding or destruction of karma.

Important Points:

  • Nirjara is of two types:
    • **Akama Nirjara (Involuntary Dissipation):** In the state of ignorance, when karmas come into उदय, their fruits are experienced, and the karma is destroyed. In this process, new karma is also bound.
    • **Sakama Nirjara (Voluntary Dissipation):** In the state of knowledge, karmas are intentionally destroyed through practices like austerities (tapa) and self-control (samyama). In this process, new karma is bound minimally or not at all.
  • Nirjara's main means is **Tapa (Austerity)**.
  • Tapa means the control or suppression of desires.
  • There are mainly 12 types of Tapa:
    • **Six External Austerities:** Anashana (fasting), Unodari (eating less than hunger), Vritti Samkshepa (limiting the number of food items), Rasa Tyaga (giving up tasty foods), Kaya Klesha (inflicting hardship on the body), Sanlinata (controlling the body's posture or withdrawing limbs).
    • **Six Internal Austerities:** Prayashchitta (repentance for sins), Vinaya (respect towards Gurus and elders), Vaiyavritya (serving Gurus, Sadhus, and elders), Svadhyaya (studying religious scriptures), Dhyana (meditation and absorption in the soul's nature), Kayotsarga (abandoning attachment to the body and remaining stable).

Practical Examples:

  • Just as water accumulated in a boat is bailed out, Nirjara dissipates the karmas bound to the soul.
  • Fasting leads to Nirjara of karmas.
  • Reading or listening to religious books is Svadhyaya Tapa, which causes Nirjara of karmas.
  • Sitting peacefully in meditation is Dhyana Tapa, which causes Nirjara of karmas.
  • Repenting for past sins and resolving not to commit them in the future is Prayashchitta Tapa.
  • Showing respect towards Guru Maharaj or elders is Vinaya Tapa.
  • Serving a sick Sadhu or a needy person is Vaiyavritya Tapa.
  • Inflicting hardship on the body through austerity (like performing Ayambil) is Kaya Klesha Tapa.

8. Bandha Tattva

Definition:

The merging of the soul's space-points (pradeshas) and karmic particles (karma raja) like milk and water is Bandha. Karmas are bound to the soul due to the result of Ashrava.

Important Points:

  • Bandha is the cause of transmigration (Samsara).
  • There are mainly four types of Karma Bandha:
    • **Prakriti Bandha (Nature Bondage):** The determination of the nature of the karma (which type of karma is bound - Gyanavaraniya, Darshanavaraniya, etc.).
    • **Sthiti Bandha (Duration Bondage):** The determination of how long the karma will remain bound to the soul.
    • **Rasa Bandha (Intensity Bondage):** The determination of the intensity (severe or mild) of the fruit of the karma.
    • **Pradesha Bandha (Space-point Bondage):** The determination of how many karmic particles will be bound to the soul.
  • Eight types of karmas are bound to the soul:
    • **Ghati Karmas (Destructive Karmas - those that obscure the soul's qualities):** Gyanavaraniya (knowledge obscuring), Darshanavaraniya (perception obscuring), Mohaniya (deluding), Antaraya (obstructing).
    • **Aghati Karmas (Non-destructive Karmas - those that give Shubha (auspicious) or Ashubha (inauspicious) fruits):** Vedaniya (feeling producing), Nama (body-making), Gotra (status-determining), Ayushya (life-span determining).

Practical Examples:

  • Just as a magnet attracts iron and sticks to it, due to the soul's impure states (vibhava bhava), karmic particles are attracted to the soul and get bound to it.
  • Lying leads to the bondage of Asatya karma (Prakriti Bandha).
  • The duration for which that karma remains bound to the soul is Sthiti Bandha.
  • The intensity of suffering that will result from the उदय (rise) of that karma is Rasa Bandha.
  • The number of karmic particles bound is Pradesha Bandha.

9. Moksha Tattva

Definition:

The complete destruction of all karmas and the manifestation of the soul's pure, inherent nature is Moksha. It is the supreme state of the Jiva.

Important Points:

  • Moksha is complete liberation from the suffering of birth and death.
  • Moksha state is endowed with infinite knowledge, infinite perception, infinite bliss, and infinite power.
  • Souls that attain Moksha are called Siddha Bhagavants, and they reside on the Siddha Shila (Abode of Liberated Souls) for infinite time.
  • There are mainly 9 types of Moksha, which represent different categories of Siddhas.
  • The main means to the path of Moksha:
    • **Samyag Darshan (Right Faith):** Accurate faith in the Tattvas.
    • **Samyag Gyan (Right Knowledge):** Accurate understanding of the Tattvas.
    • **Samyag Charitra (Right Conduct):** Conduct free from attachment and aversion, based on Samyag Gyan and Samyag Darshan (Vitaragata - state of being free from attachment and aversion).

Practical Examples:

  • Just as a boat reaches its destination and the person in it becomes safe, through Moksha, the soul crosses the fearful ocean of transmigration and attains its pure nature.
  • Just as gold is purified by heating it in fire, removing its impurities, similarly, through Tapa and Samyama, the soul removes the impurities of karmas and attains the pure state of Moksha.
  • Attaining complete destruction of all karmas after gaining Kevalgyan (omniscience) is Moksha.

Conclusion

The knowledge of the Jain Nav Tattva is the foundation of soul's welfare. By accurately understanding these Tattvas, and through the discriminative knowledge of Jiva and Ajiva, the true nature of the soul can be recognized. By knowing the nature of Punya and Papa, one can avoid Papa and engage in Punya. By considering Ashrava as Heya, the influx of karmas can be stopped through Samvara, and the bound karmas can be dissipated through Nirjara. Ultimately, by destroying all karmas, the supreme bliss of Moksha can be attained.

Deep contemplation and reflection on these nine Tattvas lead to the growth of Samyag Darshan, Gyan, and Charitra in life, and the soul can progress on the path to Moksha. By incorporating this principle expounded by the Omniscient Lords into our lives, we too can achieve the welfare of our soul.

The knowledge of the nine Tattvas is not merely a verbal understanding, but putting it into practice in life is its true fruit. When the Jiva internalizes the nature of these nine Tattvas, it understands the reality of the world. It understands that happiness and sorrow are not in external objects, but are due to its own karmas. This understanding motivates it to stay away from sinful activities and engage in auspicious activities.

By knowing the nature of Ashrava and Bandha, the Jiva tries to close the doors of karmic influx, which is Samvara. Through Samvara, the binding of new karmas is stopped. Simultaneously, through Tapa and Samyama, it dissipates the previously bound karmas (Nirjara). Through this process, the soul becomes lighter from the burden of karmas and progresses towards purity.

Finally, when the soul is completely liberated from karmas, it attains the supreme state of Moksha. This state is one of infinite bliss, knowledge, and peace. The knowledge of the nine Tattvas clearly shows this path to Moksha and guides the Jiva to reach this ultimate goal.

Thus, the Jain Nav Tattva principle is not just a religious doctrine, but it is the art of living and the path of self-development. By understanding these Tattvas and having faith in them, we can make our lives more meaningful and embark on the sacred path to Moksha.
